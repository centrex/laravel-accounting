Understanding Document Statuses

Every invoice, bill, and expense has a coloured status badge that tells you where it is in its lifecycle.

Invoice & Bill Statuses

StatusWhat It MeansWhat You Can Do Next
Draft Created but not yet sent to the ledger. No accounting impact yet. Edit the document, then click Post (invoices) or Approve (bills)
Sent Invoice has been sent to the customer but not yet formally posted. Post the invoice to record it in the accounting ledger
Issued Posted to the ledger. Customer owes the full amount. Record a payment when money is received
Partially Settled Some payment has been received; an outstanding balance remains. Record additional payments until fully settled
Settled Fully paid. Balance is zero. No further action needed
Overdue Past the due date with an unpaid balance. Record a payment or follow up with the customer/vendor
Void Cancelled. Excluded from all reports. Create a new document if needed

Expense Statuses

StatusWhat It Means
DraftNot yet posted to accounting. Click Post to record it.
ApprovedPosted as a credit (to be paid later). Click Pay when you settle it.
PartialPartially paid.
PaidFully paid. Cash was paid immediately when posted.

Journal Entry Statuses

StatusWhat It Means
DraftBeing prepared. Not yet in the ledger.
Pending ApprovalSubmitted for review by a senior accountant.
PostedFinalised and recorded in the General Ledger. Affects all reports.
VoidCancelled.
Important
Only posted transactions affect your financial reports and account balances. A draft invoice or expense has no impact until it is posted or approved.

Finding Invoices

The invoice list shows all invoices with filters at the top:

  • Search box — type an invoice number or customer name to narrow the list
  • Status filter — show only Draft, Sent, Partial, Paid, Overdue, or Void invoices
  • Date range — filter by invoice date using From and To date pickers

The list shows each invoice's number, customer, dates, total amount, remaining balance, and current status. Overdue due dates appear in red.

Creating a New Invoice

Click New Invoice — a form panel slides open.
Select a Customer from the dropdown. If the customer does not appear, add them in the Customers section first.
Set the Invoice Date and Due Date. The due date is when payment is expected.
Set the Currency (e.g. BDT). Leave as default if your customer pays in the base currency.
Add line items. Click Add Item for each product or service. Fill in Description, Quantity, Unit Price, and Tax %.
Review the totals — Subtotal, Tax, and Total are calculated automatically.
Add Notes (optional) — any extra information for the customer.
Click Create Invoice. The invoice is saved with status Draft.

Posting an Invoice to the Ledger

Creating an invoice saves it as a Draft. To record it in the accounting ledger (so it affects your AR balance and reports), you must post it.

Find the invoice in the list. It will show Draft status.
Click Post in the Actions column. A confirmation prompt appears.
Confirm. The invoice status changes to Issued and a journal entry is automatically created: the customer's Accounts Receivable increases by the invoice total.
Before you post
Double-check all amounts and the customer name. Once posted, the invoice amounts cannot be edited. If you made a mistake, void the invoice and create a new one.

Recording a Customer Payment

When a customer pays their invoice (or part of it), record the payment here.

Find the invoice — it should have status Issued, Partial, or Overdue.
Click Record Payment. A payment form appears.
Fill in the payment details (see field guide below).
Click Record Payment. The invoice status updates automatically: partial payment → Partially Settled; full payment → Settled.

Partial payments
You can record multiple partial payments against the same invoice. Each payment is stored separately with its own date and method. The invoice status will be Partially Settled until the balance reaches zero.

Adding Charges & Discounts to an Invoice

Open an invoice by clicking View, then use the buttons in the top-right of the invoice detail page.

Record Charge

Use this to add an extra cost associated with the invoice — for example, a delivery fee, courier charge, or return handling fee.

Effect: Creates an expense entry in your books. Does not increase the invoice amount owed by the customer.

Examples: Courier charge (6310), Shipping (6320), Local Delivery (6330), Return Charge (6340)

Record Discount

Use this to apply a discount or credit to the invoice — for example, a loyalty discount or goodwill adjustment.

Effect: Reduces the amount the customer owes. The outstanding balance is updated immediately.

Guard: The total of payments plus discounts cannot exceed the original invoice total.

Invoice Detail Page

Click View on any invoice to open its detail page. Here you will see:

  • Invoice summary card — customer name, dates, currency, and status badge
  • Totals card — subtotal, tax, discount, total, amount paid, and remaining balance
  • Line Items table — the individual products/services with quantities and prices
  • Payments panel — every payment recorded, with date, method, and amount
  • Journal Entries panel — the accounting transactions automatically created when the invoice was posted and when payments were received

Budgets

Planning expenditure and tracking actuals against your plan

Accounting → Budgets

Budgets let you set spending targets for specific expense accounts over a time period. Once approved, the system compares actual spending against your budget and shows you the variance.

Creating a New Budget

Click New Budget.
Give the budget a name — e.g. "Q1 2026 Operating Budget".
Set the Period — the start and end dates this budget covers.
Add budget lines — one line per account (e.g. Rent, Utilities, Salaries). For each line set the account and the budgeted amount for the period.
Click Save. Status is Draft.

Approving a Budget

A budget must be approved before it is used in variance reporting. Find the Draft budget and click Approve. Once approved, the budget cannot be deleted — create a new revised budget instead.

Viewing Budget vs Actual

Click View on any approved budget to open the variance report. For each budget line you will see:

ColumnWhat It Shows
AccountThe expense account this line covers
BudgetedThe amount you planned to spend
ActualHow much has actually been spent (based on posted transactions)
VarianceThe difference (positive = under budget, negative = over budget)
Variance %The percentage over or under
Note
Actual figures only include posted transactions within the budget's date range. Draft expenses and unposted journal entries are not counted.

Financial Reports

Generating and understanding your financial statements

Accounting → Financial Reports

The Financial Reports page lets you generate four standard accounting reports for any date range. All reports only include posted transactions.

How to Generate a Report

Select the Report Type from the dropdown (see options below).
Set the Start Date and End Date for the reporting period.
SBU Code (optional) — leave blank to include all business units, or type a code (e.g. DHAKA) to filter to one unit.
Click Generate. The report appears below.
Click Export PDF to download the report as a PDF file.

Trial Balance

A Trial Balance lists every account with its total debits and credits for the selected period. It is used to verify that the books are in balance — total debits must equal total credits.

Use it to: spot unusual account activity, verify period-end balances before closing, or prepare for an audit.

Balance Sheet

The Balance Sheet shows the financial position of your company at a single point in time (the End Date you select). It is divided into three sections:

SectionWhat It Includes
AssetsWhat the company owns — cash, bank balances, money owed by customers (AR), inventory
LiabilitiesWhat the company owes — vendor bills (AP), taxes payable, loans
EquityThe owners' stake — share capital plus retained earnings

Assets = Liabilities + Equity — if these don't balance, there is an error in the books.

Income Statement (Profit & Loss)

The Income Statement shows how much money the company made and spent over a date range. It tells you whether the business was profitable in that period.

SectionWhat It Includes
RevenueAll income earned (sales revenue)
ExpensesAll costs incurred (COGS, operating expenses, interest)
Gross ProfitRevenue minus Cost of Goods Sold
Net IncomeRevenue minus all expenses — the bottom line. Positive = profit; negative = loss.

Cash Flow Statement

The Cash Flow Statement shows where cash came from and where it went during the period. It is divided into three activities:

ActivityWhat It Tracks
OperatingCash from day-to-day business operations (collecting payments, paying suppliers)
InvestingCash spent on or received from long-term assets (equipment, property)
FinancingCash from borrowing or repaying loans, or from investors

The Net Change at the bottom shows whether your cash position improved or declined.

Which report to use?
  • Check if books are balanced → Trial Balance
  • Check what the company owns and owes right now → Balance Sheet
  • Check if the company made a profit → Income Statement
  • Check if cash is healthy despite showing a profit → Cash Flow

Journal Entries

For accountants: manually recording double-entry transactions

Accounting → Journal Entries
Accountants Only
Journal entries are for trained accountants who need to manually record transactions that don't fit the standard invoice/bill/expense workflow (e.g. depreciation, opening balances, adjustments, accruals).

Journal Entry Concepts

Every journal entry must follow the double-entry rule: the total of all debit lines must exactly equal the total of all credit lines. The system will not allow you to save an unbalanced entry.

Debit

Increases an asset or expense account.
Decreases a liability, equity, or revenue account.

Example: recording cash received → debit Cash

Credit

Increases a liability, equity, or revenue account.
Decreases an asset or expense account.

Example: recording sales income → credit Revenue

Creating a Journal Entry

Click New Entry.
Set the Entry Date, Type, Reference, and Description.
Add debit and credit lines — for each line select the account, choose Debit or Credit, and enter the amount. Add as many lines as needed.
Check the balance. The running debit and credit totals are shown. They must be equal.
Click Save — saved as Draft. Then click Post to record it in the ledger.
Journal Entry Fields
Entry Date *
The accounting date for this transaction
Type
General (default), Closing (fiscal year close), or Adjustment
Reference
Optional external document reference
Description
Explanation of what this entry records
Currency
Currency of the amounts. All amounts are stored in base currency.

Period Closing

Locking a completed month to prevent further changes

Accounting → Period Close
Irreversible Action
Closing a period is permanent. Once a period is closed, no transactions can be posted to dates within that period. Only perform this action at month-end after all transactions have been reviewed and approved.

Closing a period takes a snapshot of all account balances and locks the period. This ensures historical figures cannot be altered after the month has been reported.

Step-by-Step Period Close Process

Navigate to Period Close and select the period to close from the dropdown (e.g. "March 2026").
Click Run Pre-Close Checks. The system checks for any issues that must be resolved before closing.
Review the check results:
  • Unposted Journal Entries — shown in red. These must be posted or deleted before closing.
  • Open Invoices — shown in yellow. These are warnings only; you can close the period with open invoices.
  • Open Bills — shown in yellow. Same as above — warnings only.
Resolve all blockers (red items). Go to Journal Entries and post or delete any unposted entries within the period.
Inventory Snapshot (optional) — if the Inventory System is connected, tick the checkbox to also snapshot inventory quantities and WAC values at period-end.
Click Close Period and confirm. The period is now locked. A ✓ marker appears next to it in the dropdown.

After Closing

  • All account balances are snapshotted in the database for historical reporting.
  • Any attempt to post a transaction dated within the closed period will be rejected by the system.
  • You can still view all transactions from the closed period — they are not deleted.
  • If you need to make a correction to a closed period, contact your system administrator to discuss options.

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions and how to handle them

I created an invoice with the wrong amount. What do I do?

If the invoice is still in Draft status, you can edit it. If it has already been Posted, you must void it and create a new one. Go to the invoice detail page, scroll down to find a Void option (available to accountants), or ask your accountant to void it for you.

I recorded a payment twice by mistake. What should I do?

Contact your accountant. The system prevents exact duplicate payments (same amount, same date, same method), but if two slightly different entries were made, the accountant can investigate through the audit trail and journal entries and post a correcting entry.

An invoice shows "Overdue" but the customer has already paid. Why?

The payment has not been recorded in the system yet. Find the invoice and click Record Payment to enter the payment details. Once saved, the status will update to Settled.

I can't find a customer in the dropdown when creating an invoice.

The customer must be added to the system first. Go to Accounting → Customers, click New Customer, fill in their details, and save. They will then appear in the invoice form.

Can I export reports to Excel?

Reports can be exported to PDF using the Export PDF button. For Excel/CSV export, ask your system administrator — this may be available depending on your configuration.

What is the difference between a Bill and an Expense?

BillExpense
PurposeA formal vendor invoice you receiveAny company cost not tied to a vendor invoice
VendorMust select a registered vendorVendor is optional (free-text name)
AccountingIncreases Accounts PayableReduces Cash (cash) or increases AP (credit)
ExamplesSupplier invoice for goods purchasedOffice supplies, travel, utility bills

How do I see the history of changes to a record?

Every record has an audit trail. Click the clock icon 🕐 next to any invoice, bill, expense, or budget to see every change that was made — who made it, what changed, and when.

What does "SBU Code" mean on the Reports page?

SBU stands for Strategic Business Unit. If your organisation tracks transactions by location or department (e.g. DHAKA branch, CHITTAGONG branch), you can filter reports to one unit by typing its code. Leave it blank to see figures for the whole company.

The dashboard shows a different figure than the Income Statement. Why?

Check the date ranges. The dashboard uses the period selected in its dropdown (e.g. This Month), while the Income Statement uses the Start Date and End Date you enter manually. Make sure both cover the same period to compare figures.
